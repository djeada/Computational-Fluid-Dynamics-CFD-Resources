# Center of Mass and Centroids

The center of mass and centroid are fundamental concepts in statics that describe the average position of mass or geometric area in a body. These quantities are essential for calculating gravitational effects, analyzing structural members, and determining moments of inertia for beam and column design.

## Center of Mass

### Definition

The **center of mass** of a system of particles is the point at which the entire mass can be considered concentrated for the purpose of analyzing translational motion. For a system of $n$ discrete particles:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i}, \quad \bar{y} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i y_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i}, \quad \bar{z} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i z_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i}$$

### Continuous Bodies

For a continuous body with density $\rho$, the center of mass is:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{\int x \, dm}{\int dm} = \frac{\int x \rho \, dV}{\int \rho \, dV}$$

If the density is uniform ($\rho = \text{const}$), the center of mass coincides with the **centroid** of the volume:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{\int x \, dV}{\int dV}$$

## Centroid of Areas

### First Moment of Area

The **centroid** of a plane area $A$ is defined by:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{\int x \, dA}{\int dA} = \frac{Q_y}{A}, \quad \bar{y} = \frac{\int y \, dA}{\int dA} = \frac{Q_x}{A}$$

where $Q_x = \int y \, dA$ and $Q_y = \int x \, dA$ are the **first moments of area** about the $x$- and $y$-axes, respectively.

### Common Shapes

| Shape | Area | $\bar{x}$ | $\bar{y}$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rectangle ($b \times h$) | $bh$ | $b/2$ | $h/2$ |
| Right triangle (base $b$, height $h$) | $bh/2$ | $b/3$ | $h/3$ |
| Circle (radius $r$) | $\pi r^2$ | $0$ (center) | $0$ (center) |
| Semicircle (radius $r$) | $\pi r^2/2$ | $0$ (center) | $4r/(3\pi)$ |
| Quarter circle (radius $r$) | $\pi r^2/4$ | $4r/(3\pi)$ | $4r/(3\pi)$ |

## Centroid of Composite Shapes

### Composite Area Method

For a shape composed of several simple sub-areas $A_i$ with known centroids $(\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_i)$:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{\sum A_i \bar{x}_i}{\sum A_i}, \quad \bar{y} = \frac{\sum A_i \bar{y}_i}{\sum A_i}$$

For shapes with cutouts (holes), the removed area is treated as negative:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{A_{total}\bar{x}_{total} - A_{hole}\bar{x}_{hole}}{A_{total} - A_{hole}}$$

### Worked Example 1: T-Section Centroid

Find the centroid of a T-shaped cross section. The flange is 120 mm wide and 20 mm thick, sitting on top of a web that is 20 mm wide and 80 mm tall.

**Given:**
- Flange: $120 \times 20$ mm, centered on top of web
- Web: $20 \times 80$ mm
- Origin at the bottom-left corner of the web

**Find:** Centroid $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

**Solution:**

By symmetry, $\bar{x}$ lies on the vertical axis of symmetry. We compute $\bar{y}$ only.

Decompose into two rectangles:

**Web (rectangle 1):**
- $A_1 = 20 \times 80 = 1600$ mm²
- $\bar{y}_1 = 80/2 = 40$ mm (from bottom)

**Flange (rectangle 2):**
- $A_2 = 120 \times 20 = 2400$ mm²
- $\bar{y}_2 = 80 + 20/2 = 90$ mm (from bottom)

**Composite centroid:**
$$\bar{y} = \frac{A_1 \bar{y}_1 + A_2 \bar{y}_2}{A_1 + A_2} = \frac{1600(40) + 2400(90)}{1600 + 2400}$$
$$\bar{y} = \frac{64000 + 216000}{4000} = \frac{280000}{4000} = 70 \text{ mm}$$

The centroid is located 70 mm from the bottom of the web, along the axis of symmetry.

## Pappus–Guldinus Theorems

These theorems relate surface areas and volumes of revolution to centroids of generating curves and areas.

### First Theorem (Surface Area)

The surface area of a body of revolution is equal to the generating curve length $L$ multiplied by the distance traveled by its centroid:

$$A_{surface} = 2\pi \bar{y} L$$

where $\bar{y}$ is the distance from the centroid of the curve to the axis of revolution.

### Second Theorem (Volume)

The volume of a body of revolution is equal to the generating area $A$ multiplied by the distance traveled by its centroid:

$$V = 2\pi \bar{y} A$$

### Example: Torus

A torus is generated by revolving a circle of radius $r$ about an axis at distance $R$ from the circle's center ($R > r$):

$$V_{torus} = 2\pi R \cdot \pi r^2 = 2\pi^2 R r^2$$
$$A_{torus} = 2\pi R \cdot 2\pi r = 4\pi^2 R r$$

## Moment of Inertia (Second Moment of Area)

### Definition

The **second moment of area** (often called moment of inertia in structural contexts) quantifies how an area is distributed relative to an axis. For an area $A$:

$$I_x = \int y^2 \, dA, \quad I_y = \int x^2 \, dA$$

The moment of inertia determines a beam's resistance to bending — a larger $I$ means greater stiffness and strength.

### Common Shapes

| Shape | $I_x$ (about centroidal axis) |
|---|---|
| Rectangle ($b \times h$) | $\frac{bh^3}{12}$ |
| Circle (radius $r$) | $\frac{\pi r^4}{4}$ |
| Hollow circle ($r_o$, $r_i$) | $\frac{\pi}{4}(r_o^4 - r_i^4)$ |
| Triangle (base $b$, height $h$) | $\frac{bh^3}{36}$ |

### Polar Moment of Inertia

The polar moment of inertia about the $z$-axis (perpendicular to the area) is:

$$J_O = I_x + I_y = \int (x^2 + y^2) \, dA = \int r^2 \, dA$$

This quantity is important for torsion analysis of circular shafts.

## Parallel Axis Theorem

### Statement

The moment of inertia about any axis parallel to a centroidal axis is:

$$I = I_c + Ad^2$$

where:
- $I_c$ = moment of inertia about the centroidal axis
- $A$ = area of the shape
- $d$ = perpendicular distance between the two parallel axes

This theorem is essential for computing the moment of inertia of composite cross sections.

### Worked Example 2: Composite I-Beam

Compute the moment of inertia $I_x$ about the centroidal horizontal axis of an I-beam with the following dimensions: two flanges of $150 \times 15$ mm and a web of $10 \times 170$ mm. The total depth is 200 mm.

**Given:**
- Top flange: $150 \times 15$ mm, centered at $\bar{y}_{tf} = 200 - 7.5 = 192.5$ mm from bottom
- Web: $10 \times 170$ mm, centered at $\bar{y}_w = 15 + 85 = 100$ mm from bottom
- Bottom flange: $150 \times 15$ mm, centered at $\bar{y}_{bf} = 7.5$ mm from bottom

**Find:** $I_x$ about the centroidal axis.

**Solution:**

By symmetry, the centroid of the entire section is at $\bar{y} = 100$ mm (mid-depth).

**Bottom flange:**
$$I_{c,bf} = \frac{150 \times 15^3}{12} = 42187.5 \text{ mm}^4$$
$$d_{bf} = 100 - 7.5 = 92.5 \text{ mm}$$
$$I_{bf} = 42187.5 + (150 \times 15)(92.5)^2 = 42187.5 + 19,253,437.5 = 19,295,625 \text{ mm}^4$$

**Web:**
$$I_w = \frac{10 \times 170^3}{12} = 4,094,167 \text{ mm}^4$$
(Centroid of web coincides with the section centroid, so $d = 0$.)

**Top flange** (same as bottom by symmetry):
$$I_{tf} = 19,295,625 \text{ mm}^4$$

**Total:**
$$I_x = I_{bf} + I_w + I_{tf} = 19,295,625 + 4,094,167 + 19,295,625$$
$$I_x = 42,685,417 \text{ mm}^4 \approx 4.27 \times 10^7 \text{ mm}^4$$

## Applications

### Beam Design
- The **section modulus** $S = I/c$ determines the bending stress capacity: $\sigma = M/S$
- Selecting efficient cross sections (I-beams, channels) maximizes $I$ for minimum material
- The centroid location determines the neutral axis in bending

### Column Buckling
- The critical buckling load depends on the minimum moment of inertia:
$$P_{cr} = \frac{\pi^2 EI_{min}}{(KL)^2}$$
- Designing columns with adequate $I$ in all directions prevents premature buckling

### Fluid Mechanics
- The centroid of a submerged surface determines the location of the resultant hydrostatic force
- The second moment of area determines the center of pressure offset:
$$y_{cp} - \bar{y} = \frac{I_c}{A\bar{y}}$$

### Machine Design
- Polar moment of inertia governs torsional stiffness of shafts
- Mass moment of inertia determines rotational dynamics of flywheels and gears

## Practical Tips

- Use symmetry to reduce calculations: the centroid lies on any axis of symmetry
- For composite shapes, create a systematic table with columns for $A_i$, $\bar{y}_i$, $A_i\bar{y}_i$, $I_{c,i}$, $d_i$, and $A_i d_i^2$
- Always apply the parallel axis theorem when transferring moments of inertia to a non-centroidal axis
- Verify results by checking units: area moments have units of length to the fourth power (mm⁴ or m⁴)
- When subtracting holes or cutouts, remember to subtract both the centroidal $I_c$ and the transfer term $Ad^2$

Understanding centroids and moments of inertia is indispensable for structural analysis and design. These geometric properties connect the shape of a cross section to its mechanical performance under bending, torsion, and buckling loads.
